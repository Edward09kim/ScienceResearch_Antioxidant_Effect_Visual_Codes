Sea Cucumber Exosome Antioxidant Research

Exploring antioxidant potential of exosome-like vesicles

Abstract

This study investigated whether exosome-like vesicles extracted from sea cucumbers possess antioxidant activity. 
Exosomes were isolated through homogenization, centrifugation, and filtration. Antioxidant activity was evaluated 
using a DPPH radical scavenging assay. The results showed concentration-dependent antioxidant activity, suggesting 
potential applications in anti-aging research and space biotechnology.

Introduction

Aging is closely related to oxidative damage caused by reactive oxygen species (ROS). Excess ROS damages 
mitochondria and accelerates cellular aging. This effect can become more severe in extreme environments 
such as space, where astronauts experience accelerated aging due to microgravity.

Exosomes are nanoscale extracellular vesicles that transport proteins, lipids, and other bioactive molecules 
between cells. Because of their biological function, exosomes may carry antioxidant compounds.

Sea cucumbers are marine organisms known for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. 
This study hypothesizes that exosome-like vesicles extracted from sea cucumbers may possess antioxidant 
activity capable of reducing oxidative stress.

Materials and Methods

Sea cucumbers were mechanically homogenized and filtered to remove large debris. The solution was centrifuged 
to remove cellular impurities and sequentially filtered using 0.45 μm and 0.22 μm filters to isolate 
exosome-like vesicles.

Antioxidant activity was measured using a DPPH radical scavenging assay. Trolox served as the positive control 
and ethanol served as the negative control. Samples were incubated with DPPH solution for 30 minutes and 
absorbance was measured at 517 nm.

Results

The DPPH assay demonstrated concentration-dependent antioxidant activity in sea cucumber exosome extracts. 
Samples treated with higher concentrations showed lower absorbance values, indicating stronger radical 
scavenging activity compared with the control group.

These results suggest that compounds present in the sea cucumber extract are capable of neutralizing 
free radicals.

Discussion

The experiment demonstrated that sea cucumber–derived exosome extracts possess measurable antioxidant activity. 
When mixed with DPPH solution, the purple radical solution changed to yellow, indicating that antioxidant 
components donated electrons and neutralized free radicals.

Sea cucumbers naturally experience oxidative stress in marine environments due to UV radiation, salinity 
changes, and reactive oxygen species. As a result, they have developed strong antioxidant defense systems. 
Exosomes may function as carriers that transport antioxidant molecules produced by sea cucumber cells.

However, the extraction process used in this study did not fully isolate pure exosomes. Therefore, the 
observed antioxidant activity may result from both extracellular vesicles and other bioactive compounds 
present in the extract. Future research should include more advanced purification and molecular analysis 
to confirm the specific role of sea cucumber exosomes.

Conclusion

This study provides preliminary evidence that exosome-like vesicles extracted from sea cucumbers show 
antioxidant activity in a DPPH radical scavenging assay. As the concentration increased from 5 µg/mL 
to 20 µg/mL, radical scavenging activity also increased, indicating a concentration-dependent effect.

These findings suggest that sea cucumber–derived exosomes may serve as a natural source of antioxidant 
compounds. In the future, such biological materials could contribute to the development of antioxidant 
supplements, biomedical therapies, and potential applications in space biotechnology to help protect 
cells from oxidative stress.
