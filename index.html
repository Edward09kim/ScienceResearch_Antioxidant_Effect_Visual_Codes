Sea Cucumber Exosome Antioxidant Research

Abstract

This study aimed to investigate whether exosome-like vesicles extracted from sea cucumber
exhibit antioxidant activity. To address this research question, exosomes were isolated from
frozen sea cucumber tissue through homogenization, sequential centrifugation, and filtration
using 0.45 μm and 0.22 μm filters. The antioxidant activity of the extracted exosomes was then
evaluated using a DPPH radical scavenging assay.

Reactive oxygen species, also known as ROS, are a main cause of cellular aging since they
harm mitochondria, disrupting energy production mechanisms, and accelerating human aging.

In the context of space, where astronauts experience microgravity, accelerated aging
transformations such as muscle atrophy and bone loss occur.

This research aims to determine whether exosomes generated by sea cucumbers have
antioxidant properties that could assist in degrading cellular damage generated by ROS.

Introduction

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) play a critical role in biological aging processes.
ROS damage cellular structures including proteins, lipids, and DNA.

Marine organisms have recently attracted attention due to their bioactive compounds.
Sea cucumbers, in particular, contain peptides and compounds that exhibit antioxidant
and anti-inflammatory properties.

Exosomes are nano-sized extracellular vesicles that facilitate cell-to-cell communication
and may carry proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids.

Understanding whether sea cucumber derived exosomes possess antioxidant properties
may open new directions for biomedical applications related to aging and oxidative stress.

Methods

Frozen sea cucumber tissue was homogenized to extract cellular components.

The homogenized samples underwent sequential centrifugation to remove large debris
and isolate smaller vesicles.

Filtration was performed using 0.45 μm and 0.22 μm membrane filters.

The isolated vesicles were considered exosome-like structures.

To evaluate antioxidant activity, the DPPH radical scavenging assay was conducted.
DPPH is a stable free radical compound commonly used to measure antioxidant capacity.

When antioxidants are present, the purple DPPH solution becomes yellow,
indicating radical scavenging activity.

Results

The experimental results indicated a visible color change during the DPPH assay.

The purple DPPH solution turned yellow after interaction with the extracted
sea cucumber exosome solution.

This color change suggests that the exosome extract contains compounds
capable of neutralizing free radicals.

Therefore, the presence of antioxidant activity in the extracted vesicles
was confirmed.

Discussion

The results support the hypothesis that sea cucumber derived exosomes
exhibit antioxidant activity.

The DPPH assay demonstrated clear radical scavenging capability.

These findings align with previous studies that describe bioactive peptides
in sea cucumbers that exhibit antioxidant effects.

Exosomes may act as carriers for these bioactive compounds, potentially
enhancing their biological availability.

Such mechanisms could be valuable in addressing oxidative stress related
to aging or extreme environments such as space.

Conclusion

This experiment demonstrated that exosome-like vesicles extracted from
sea cucumber exhibit measurable antioxidant activity.

The DPPH radical scavenging assay confirmed the ability of these vesicles
to neutralize free radicals.

These findings suggest potential biomedical applications of sea cucumber
derived exosomes in combating oxidative stress and cellular aging.
