Sea Cucumber Exosome Antioxidant Research

Exploring antioxidant potential of exosome-like vesicles
Preliminary Report
ScienceResearch

Abstract

This study aimed to investigate whether exosome-like vesicles extracted from sea cucumber
exhibit antioxidant activity. Exosomes were isolated from frozen sea cucumber tissue through
homogenization, sequential centrifugation, and filtration. Antioxidant activity was evaluated
using a DPPH radical scavenging assay. The results demonstrated concentration-dependent
antioxidant activity, suggesting potential applications in anti-aging and space biotechnology.

Introduction

Aging is a fundamental biological process closely associated with cellular damage and declining
physiological function. One major contributor to cellular aging is the accumulation of reactive
oxygen species (ROS), which are inevitably generated during mitochondrial energy production.
Mitochondria produce approximately 95% of cellular energy required for protein synthesis;
however, excessive ROS damages mitochondrial structures, reducing energy efficiency and
accelerating aging.

This phenomenon is intensified in extreme environments such as space. Astronauts experience
accelerated aging due to microgravity, including muscle atrophy, bone loss, and metabolic
alterations. Studies involving identical twins, such as NASA astronauts Scott Kelly and Mark Kelly,
have demonstrated biological changes during long-duration spaceflight, including alterations in
telomere length and mitochondrial function.

One potential solution to oxidative stress is the use of exosomes. Exosomes are nanoscale
extracellular vesicles released by cells for intercellular communication, often containing
bioactive molecules such as proteins and lipids. Due to their biological role, exosomes may
possess tissue-repairing and antioxidant capabilities.

Sea cucumbers are marine organisms known for their antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and
restorative properties. Previous studies have reported strong antioxidant activity in sea
cucumber-derived peptides. This study hypothesizes that exosome-like vesicles extracted from
sea cucumbers possess antioxidant properties capable of reducing ROS-induced cellular damage.

Materials and Methods

Purified sea cucumbers were mechanically homogenized using a magnetic stirrer until a
semi-liquid consistency was achieved. The homogenized mixture was filtered through sterile
gauze to remove large debris and centrifuged at 3,000 × g for 10 minutes to remove cellular
impurities. The supernatant was further centrifuged at 10,000 × g for 30 minutes.

The resulting solution was sequentially filtered using 0.45 μm and 0.22 μm syringe filters to
isolate exosome-like vesicles. The final filtrate was collected for antioxidant analysis.

Antioxidant activity was evaluated using a DPPH radical scavenging assay. Trolox was used as
a standard antioxidant reference, while 99% ethanol served as a negative control. Samples
were incubated with DPPH solution for 30 minutes in the dark, and absorbance was measured
at 517 nm.

Results

The DPPH assay demonstrated concentration-dependent antioxidant activity in sea cucumber
exosome extracts. The negative control exhibited an absorbance value of approximately 0.46.
Samples treated with 5 μg/mL and 20 μg/mL exosome extracts showed reduced absorbance values
of approximately 0.38 and 0.28, respectively.

The calculated radical scavenging activity was approximately 16% for the low concentration
group and 38% for the high concentration group. These values were comparable to Trolox
standards, indicating effective antioxidant capacity.

Discussion

The results confirm that sea cucumber-derived exosome-like vesicles exhibit antioxidant activity.
The observed color change in the DPPH assay reflects effective redox reactions in which
antioxidant components donate electrons to neutralize free radicals.

Sea cucumbers naturally endure oxidative stress in marine environments, which may explain
the presence of potent antioxidant systems. Exosomes likely function as carriers of these
bioactive molecules. However, further purification and molecular characterization are required
to confirm that the antioxidant activity is exclusively derived from exosomes.

Conclusion

This study demonstrates that exosome-like vesicles extracted from sea cucumbers possess
significant antioxidant activity. The concentration-dependent effects observed in the DPPH
assay suggest potential applications in anti-aging research, space biotechnology, and
biomedical antioxidant development.
