Sea Cucumber Exosome Antioxidant Research

Investigating the Antioxidant Potential of Exosome-Like Vesicles Derived from Sea Cucumber

Edited by Chanyu Edward Kim & Daniel Eungyeol Park

Abstract

This study aimed to investigate whether exosome-like vesicles extracted from sea cucumber exhibit antioxidant activity. Exosomes were isolated from frozen sea cucumber tissue through homogenization, centrifugation, and filtration using 0.45 μm and 0.22 μm filters. Antioxidant activity was evaluated using a DPPH radical scavenging assay. Reactive oxygen species (ROS) are a major cause of cellular aging because they damage mitochondria and disrupt energy production.

The results showed concentration-dependent antioxidant activity. At 5 µg/mL the inhibition rate was approximately 16%, while at 20 µg/mL it increased to about 38%. These findings suggest that sea cucumber exosomes may help reduce oxidative stress and slow cellular aging.

Introduction

Human cells require energy to synthesize proteins that form tissues such as bones, skin, organs, and muscles. This energy is produced by mitochondria, which generate approximately 95% of the cell’s energy supply. However, during mitochondrial energy production reactive oxygen species (ROS) are inevitably generated.

ROS can damage mitochondria and disrupt cellular processes, ultimately accelerating the aging process. Because of this, removing ROS has become an important scientific challenge in aging research.

One promising biological solution is the use of exosomes. Exosomes are extracellular vesicles released by cells that help with intercellular communication and tissue repair. Sea cucumbers are known to contain strong antioxidant peptides and bioactive compounds. Therefore, exosomes derived from sea cucumbers may contain compounds capable of reducing oxidative stress.

This research was also inspired by the NASA Twins Study involving Scott Kelly and Mark Kelly. Because astronauts experience microgravity, they undergo accelerated aging processes such as muscle atrophy and bone density loss. Understanding antioxidant mechanisms may therefore contribute to future biomedical solutions for astronauts.

Materials and Methods

Exosome Extraction

Sea cucumber tissue was homogenized using a magnetic stirrer and filtered to remove large debris. The solution underwent multiple centrifugation steps to remove cellular components and impurities. The supernatant was sequentially filtered using 0.45 μm and 0.22 μm filters to obtain extracellular vesicle fractions containing exosome-like vesicles.

DPPH Antioxidant Assay

Antioxidant activity was measured using a DPPH radical scavenging assay. Trolox, a water-soluble analog of vitamin E, was used as a standard antioxidant reference. Samples were incubated in a 96-well microplate with DPPH solution for 30 minutes before measuring absorbance at 517 nm.

Results

The negative control absorbance value was measured at approximately 0.46. Samples treated with sea cucumber exosome extract showed lower absorbance values compared with the control.

At 5 µg/mL the radical scavenging activity was approximately 16%, while at 20 µg/mL it increased to approximately 38%. These results indicate that antioxidant activity increases as the concentration of exosomes increases.

The antioxidant activity observed in the samples was comparable to Trolox reference standards of 40 µg/mL and 80 µg/mL.

Discussion

The results demonstrate that sea cucumber exosome extracts possess measurable antioxidant properties. The DPPH solution changed color from purple to yellow, indicating the presence of antioxidant compounds capable of donating electrons to neutralize free radicals.

Sea cucumbers naturally live in environments with high oxidative stress, including exposure to ultraviolet radiation and reactive oxygen. Because of this environment, they have evolved strong antioxidant defense mechanisms.

These antioxidant compounds may be transported through extracellular vesicles such as exosomes. The results suggest that such biological materials may potentially contribute to reducing oxidative stress in cells.

Further purification and biochemical analysis would be necessary to determine whether the antioxidant activity originates specifically from exosomes or other bioactive compounds present in the extract.

Conclusion

This study demonstrates that exosome-like vesicles derived from sea cucumbers exhibit measurable antioxidant activity in a DPPH radical scavenging assay. The activity increases with higher concentrations of exosome extract.

These findings suggest that sea cucumber exosomes may serve as a natural antioxidant resource and could potentially contribute to future biomedical applications, including anti-aging therapies and space biotechnology.
